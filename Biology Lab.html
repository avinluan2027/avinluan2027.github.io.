Laboratory Report

Dive into the fascinating world of the digestive system with our interactive simulation! In this lab report, we'll guide you through the key aspects of our simulation , where you'll witness the intricate processes that occur when you consume food.

Step 1 -Introduction

All organisms are required to digest their food to obtain energy, which allows cellular operations and creates chemicals required for equilibrium and life. In our school, we talk about how food impacts and responds to our bodies. Digestion happens when we consume food, which has a direct and long-term impact on our body. This could be either a negative outcome or a healthy one. As a result, choosing a nutritious meal necessitates understanding what is in your food, which is important for your health. Proteins, lipids, carbohydrates, and nucleic acids are four macromolecules that may be used to roughly characterize the food we eat. With this simulation exercise, we will obtain a visual grasp of how the digestive system works, which will aid us in understanding our digestive system.

Step 3 -Documenting the Stages of Food Processing

During the crushing step, we saw that the zip-lock bag represented the stomach. The banana and soda crackers may represent food going from the esophagus into the stomach. The water represented gastric juice, and the meal was subsequently transformed into what is commonly referred to as chyme. The crushing of the food within the zip-lock bag represents food breakdown or digestion. The enzymes from the parietal and chief cells are represented by the orange or lemon juice, which is mixed with the chyme. The soda crackers and banana were then crushed by squeezing the zip-lock bag to break down the meal. We infer that the biomolecule, protein, is one of the components of the food inside the zip-lock bag. Given that the gastric juice contains pepsin, a protease, and hydrochloric acid, it assists in protein digestion. The plastic cup and stocking are used during the process of transferring. This represents the pyloric sphincter, which acts as a muscle valve that opens to allow food to pass from the stomach to the top of the small intestine. The fluid escaping the stocking indicates chyme entering the small intestine's duodenum. After that, the chyme goes to the small intestine after being broken down in the stomach, and in this case, the small intestine is represented by the tray. Most of the food was in bits that hadn't been fully crushed. This could represent food being churned until it passes through the pyloric sphincter.

Step 4 -Documenting Simulation of Absorption

The paper towel with the greatest folds absorbed the most water, which could represent our small intestine. The absorption of water from paper towels is represented by the ileum and jejunum, which handle the bulk of the absorption stage. The small intestine is long and coated in microscopic projections called villi. This increases the surface area available for food absorption and enzymatic secretion. Chyme is joined here with pancreatic, liver, and gallbladder secretions, as well as gland cells in the intestines. Fructose is taken up by the intestinal lumen and transported to the epithelial cells via the increased diffusion mechanism. Other molecules that are actively transported include most glucose molecules, small peptides, amino acids, and vitamins. After passing through the microvilli of the epithelial cells, the capillary bed is positioned in the middle of the villi. Fat absorption and transportation, on the other hand, are distinct. Bile must emulsify the particles before lipase can perform. Lipase's breakdown will produce fatty acids and a monoglyceride as a byproduct. The epithelial cells can now absorb them.

Step 5 -Documenting Simulation of Bile Action

The liver produces bile, which is temporarily held in the gallbladder before being discharged along with pancreatic secretion. Bile is amphipathic because it has both a hydrophilic and a hydrophobic side. They dissolve large fat molecules into smaller units known as micelles, acting as emulsifiers. As these micelles join, their hydrophilic part confronts the aqueous chyme. Bile is not a digestive enzyme. It is merely a solvent used to emulsify fats. However, since it still helps in breaking down fats, it is considered a chemical digestion. Furthermore, increasing the surface area-to-volume ratio of the small intestine will further help the enzymes break down fats.
